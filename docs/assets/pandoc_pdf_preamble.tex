\newcommand{\verbxChronRow}[2]{#1 & #2\tabularnewline\noalign{\vskip 7pt}}
\newcommand{\verbxChronology}{%
  \chapter*{Chronology of Reverberation}
  \addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Chronology of Reverberation}
  This selective chronology is a historical reading map, not a story in which one
  technology neatly replaces another. Architecture, chambers, plates, springs, magnetic
  delay, digital networks, convolution, physical modeling, spatial rendering, and learned
  systems continue to coexist because each stores, shapes, and spatializes acoustic energy
  differently. Dates identify useful landmarks; broad periods acknowledge practices whose
  origins are distributed across cultures, institutions, patents, laboratories, studios,
  compositions, and oral engineering traditions.

  Each entry asks three questions. What medium stores the acoustic memory? Which controls
  become available to a musician or engineer? Which assumptions become hidden? Read the
  chronology alongside Chapter 2's illustrated history and Appendix E's research
  bibliography, where primary papers, patents, institutional histories, and surveys supply
  fuller attribution and technical context.

  \begingroup
  \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.10}
  \begin{longtable}{@{}p{0.18\textwidth}p{0.72\textwidth}@{}}
    \verbxChronRow{Prehistory}{Caves, rock shelters, canyons, and other resonant sites join
      sound to place long before acoustics exists as a named science. Repetition, sustained
      vocalization, percussion, and movement reveal echoes and modes empirically. Modern
      archaeoacoustics studies these relationships cautiously, combining measurement with
      archaeological and cultural evidence rather than treating resonance alone as proof
      of historical intention.}
    \verbxChronRow{Classical antiquity}{Open-air theaters, enclosed ritual spaces, and
      monumental stone architecture organize projection and reflection through geometry.
      Builders and performers work with audibility, speech rhythm, ensemble placement, and
      audience distribution without reducing the result to one decay number. Direct sound,
      early support, and intelligibility already form a practical design problem.}
    \verbxChronRow{c. 20 BCE}{Vitruvius's \emph{De architectura} describes theater geometry,
      voice projection, and resonant bronze vessels associated with Greek performance
      practice. The account mixes observation, inherited theory, and idealized prescription,
      but it is an early written attempt to connect built form, resonant frequency, and
      intelligibility. Modern readers should resist treating the vessels as electronic
      equalizers in disguise: the larger historical fact is that architecture and tuned
      resonance were already discussed as parts of one system.}
    \verbxChronRow{Sixth century}{Hagia Sophia, completed in 537, demonstrates the acoustic
      consequences of a vast central dome joined to semidomes, galleries, columns, and
      reflective masonry. Its field is neither a simple rectangular decay nor a uniform
      diffuse bath. Long paths, coupled volumes, focusing, and changing occupancy make the
      building a continuing case study in architectural and liturgical sound.}
    \verbxChronRow{Medieval sacred architecture}{Romanesque and Gothic churches enlarge
      vertical volume, multiply bays and aisles, and place sung text within long, spectrally
      evolving decays. Chant and early polyphony are shaped by breath, syllabic pacing,
      processional movement, and architecture. The room is not an effect added afterward;
      it is part of performance timing and collective intonation.}
    \verbxChronRow{c. 800--1200}{Maya ceremonial architecture supplies a distinct history of
      reflection and resonance. At Chich\'en Itz\'a, handclaps near the stairway of El Castillo
      can return as a descending, chirp-like echo because the stepped geometry disperses
      frequency components in time. Archaeoacoustic study compares measurements, models,
      architecture, and cultural evidence; the striking sound does not by itself establish
      that builders intended to imitate a bird. The example remains an unusually clear lesson
      in how repeated geometry transforms a transient.}
    \verbxChronRow{c. 1365}{Guillaume de Machaut's \emph{Messe de Nostre Dame} (c. 1365)
      illustrates how sustained lines, staggered syllables, and contrapuntal density can
      cooperate with a resonant church. Overlap joins phrases, but excessive late energy
      can erase textual and rhythmic distinctions. The musical problem already resembles
      modern reverb mixing: continuity must not destroy articulation.}
    \verbxChronRow{1436}{Guillaume Dufay's \emph{Nuper rosarum flores} (1436) was performed
      for the consecration of Florence Cathedral. Its ceremonial pacing, alternating forces,
      and sustained sonorities demonstrate how composition, procession, and monumental
      architecture can be heard as one event. Claims that every musical proportion directly
      encodes the cathedral remain debated, but the performance history still offers a useful
      reminder: acoustic space affects formal scale, ensemble deployment, and the listener's
      perception of arrival.}
    \verbxChronRow{Sixteenth century}{Large ceremonial vocal repertories increasingly use
      separated groups, registral contrast, and antiphonal exchange. Thomas Tallis's
      \emph{Spem in alium} (c. 1570) places forty independent parts in a texture whose
      intelligibility depends on attack spacing, ensemble geometry, and the room's ability
      to unify without collapsing many lines into one spectral mass.}
    \verbxChronRow{Late sixteenth--early seventeenth centuries}{Venetian polychoral practice
      makes architectural separation explicitly compositional. Giovanni Gabrieli's
      \emph{In ecclesiis} (c. 1615) coordinates voices, instruments, balconies, and shared
      decay. Local early reflections help distinguish groups while the late field connects
      them, anticipating modern object placement feeding a common immersive reverberator.}
    \verbxChronRow{1650}{Athanasius Kircher's \emph{Musurgia universalis} (1650) gathers
      contemporary ideas about echo, speaking tubes, resonant spaces, instruments, and the
      transmission of sound. Its engravings are as important historically as its uneven
      explanations: they show acoustics becoming a visual and mechanical subject whose paths
      can be drawn, redirected, and demonstrated. The book belongs to a long prehistory of
      signal-flow thinking, even though its categories differ sharply from modern linear
      systems and measured impulse responses.}
    \verbxChronRow{Seventeenth--eighteenth centuries}{Opera houses, court theaters, chapels,
      and purpose-built concert rooms develop alongside changing ensemble sizes and public
      listening practices. Designers reason through proportion, materials, ornament,
      occupancy, and empirical adjustment. Musicians adapt tempo, articulation, scoring,
      continuo, and dynamics to the response of particular rooms.}
    \verbxChronRow{Nineteenth century}{Industrial materials, larger halls, orchestral
      expansion, public organs, and new audience expectations intensify questions of power,
      blend, clarity, and reverberant support. Individual resonances and coupled volumes
      remain audible, but statistical thinking becomes increasingly attractive as rooms
      grow and measurement practices mature.}
    \verbxChronRow{1877--1890s}{The phonograph, improved microphones, telephone circuits,
      and early electrical transmission alter the meaning of acoustic memory. A sound can be
      stored, replayed, transmitted, and auditioned away from its originating room. Recording
      initially captures reverberation as an inseparable trace, but repeatability makes
      comparison possible: performers and engineers can hear how placement, horn geometry,
      enclosure, and medium change the balance between direct energy and reflected sound.}
    \verbxChronRow{1890s}{Wallace Clement Sabine's work connects room volume, effective
      absorption, and reverberation time through repeatable experiments. Reverberation
      becomes a quantity that can be estimated, compared, and designed, while the limits of
      the diffuse-field assumption remain important. The resulting framework establishes
      a durable bridge between architectural choices and temporal decay.}
    \verbxChronRow{1900}{Boston Symphony Hall opens after Sabine advises its design. The hall
      becomes a prominent demonstration that architectural acoustics can combine calculation,
      material specification, model reasoning, and musical judgment before construction is
      complete. Its history also cautions against reducing quality to reverberation time:
      lateral energy, stage support, frequency balance, occupancy, geometry, and performer
      experience all contribute to the result listeners call a successful room.}
    \verbxChronRow{Early twentieth century}{Telephone, radio, electroacoustic transducers,
      electrical recording, and amplification separate sound production from immediate
      acoustic radiation. Once microphones and loudspeakers can connect distant spaces, a
      room can become a signal-processing component. The conceptual ingredients of an
      effects send, remote chamber, and returned ambience begin to align.}
    \verbxChronRow{1920s}{Electrical recording improves bandwidth and permits microphones,
      amplifiers, loudspeakers, and mixers to participate more directly in spatial design.
      Engineers can favor controlled capture and then restore a chosen sense of space.
      Reverberation starts to become separable from the original performance location,
      though editing and routing remain physically cumbersome.}
    \verbxChronRow{1930}{Carl F. Eyring publishes a reverberation relation that improves on
      the Sabine approximation when average absorption is not small. The distinction is more
      than a competing formula: it exposes the assumptions behind a decay estimate and the
      difference between discrete energy loss at boundaries and a continuous exponential
      model. Later software can compute a number instantly, but responsible interpretation
      still depends on field diffuseness, source-receiver placement, bandwidth, and fit range.}
    \verbxChronRow{1930s}{Radio, sound film, public-address work, and electrical organs
      create demand for compact or repeatable ambience. Stairwells, tiled rooms, tanks,
      pipes, acoustic labyrinths, magnetic paths, and resonant objects are evaluated as
      practical devices. Noise, isolation, flutter, transducer placement, and bandwidth
      become engineering constraints as important as nominal decay time.}
    \verbxChronRow{1939--1941}{Laurens Hammond's spring-reverberation patent documents a
      compact electromechanical route for extending an electrical instrument's decay.
      Torsional waves, transducers, dispersion, and mechanical damping create a response
      unlike a miniature room. The spring's audible identity later becomes central to organ,
      guitar, surf, dub, studio, and experimental practices.}
    \verbxChronRow{1940s}{Studios increasingly treat reflective rooms as outboard processors.
      A console send drives a loudspeaker in the chamber; microphones capture the response;
      equalization, compression, placement, and return level shape the result. This topology
      remains recognizable in every modern auxiliary reverb bus even after the physical
      room is replaced by code.}
    \verbxChronRow{1947}{Bill Putnam's work on the Harmonicats' \emph{Peg o' My Heart}
      (1947) is frequently cited as an early artistic landmark for chamber reverberation in
      record production. The important shift is authorship: reflected sound is deliberately
      captured, balanced, and printed as a production layer rather than accepted only as an
      inseparable property of the recording venue.}
    \verbxChronRow{Late 1940s--1950s}{Magnetic tape makes delay, repetition, reversal, varispeed,
      splicing, and feedback available as compositional operations. Tape echo is not diffuse
      reverberation, yet recirculating repeats can be filtered, saturated, offset, and layered
      until they function as an artificial acoustic field. Reversing a printed wet signal
      creates the rising envelope later associated with reverse reverb, while a gate can turn
      an otherwise continuous decay into a precisely timed rhythmic event.}
    \verbxChronRow{1950s}{Purpose-built studio chambers turn architecture into repeatable
      infrastructure. Major facilities develop identifiable spatial signatures, while
      engineers learn to vary apparent size and brightness through loudspeaker position,
      microphone pattern, distance, filtering, and return dynamics. Chambers are expensive
      and fixed, but their physical complexity is difficult to duplicate exactly.}
    \verbxChronRow{1957}{The EMT 140 plate reverberator stores energy in a driven steel
      sheet and captures vibration with pickups. It supplies dense, bright decay without a
      room-sized air volume and allows mechanical damping to shorten the tail. Plate sound
      becomes both a technical solution and a production category, especially effective on
      voices, drums, and sustained instruments.}
    \verbxChronRow{Late 1950s--1960s}{Springs, plates, chambers, tape delay, magnetic drums,
      pipes, folded acoustic paths, and electronic feedback coexist. No single device wins;
      each exposes a different compromise among size, cost, bandwidth, noise, density,
      portability, controllability, and coloration. Studio practice develops around
      combining these media rather than pursuing one universal room simulation.}
    \verbxChronRow{1960}{Harry F. Olson and John C. Bleazey describe a synthetic
      reverberator using loudspeaker, pipe, microphone, and feedback. The system makes the
      cross-domain abstraction visible: propagation supplies delay, the path supplies
      frequency-dependent loss, transducers cross between acoustic and electrical domains,
      and feedback extends effective decay.}
    \verbxChronRow{1961}{Manfred R. Schroeder and Benjamin F. Logan formalize
      \textquotedblleft{}colorless\textquotedblright{}
      artificial reverberation using parallel feedback comb filters and serial allpass
      sections. Delay lengths, loop gains, mode distribution, echo density, and spectral
      coloration become analyzable design variables. The topology establishes a vocabulary
      still heard in software reverberators and teaching diagrams.}
    \verbxChronRow{1961--1962}{Schroeder's related work on natural-sounding artificial
      reverberation develops the perceptual argument: exact simulation of every reflection
      is unnecessary when a compact system reproduces the statistics listeners use. This
      reframes reverberator design as a negotiated relation among physical plausibility,
      computational economy, coloration, density, and auditory expectation.}
    \verbxChronRow{1960s measurement practice}{Warble tones, interrupted noise, pistol shots,
      balloon bursts, spark sources, and loudspeaker-driven test signals support increasingly
      repeatable room measurements. Each excitation reveals a different compromise among
      bandwidth, directivity, crest factor, portability, repeatability, and signal-to-noise
      ratio. The shift toward measured decay curves encourages octave-band reporting and
      separates source behavior, room response, microphone response, and background noise as
      distinct parts of an experimental chain.}
    \verbxChronRow{1960s computer music}{Digital delay and filter ideas enter computer-music
      research before economical commercial realtime hardware is widespread. Offline and
      institutional systems permit experiments with recursion, stochastic structures,
      spatial projection, and durations impractical in electromechanical devices. Reverb
      becomes compositional code as well as room simulation.}
    \verbxChronRow{Late 1960s--early 1970s}{Integrated circuits, converters, semiconductor
      memory, and digital control improve steadily, but high-quality realtime delay remains
      costly. Designers work under severe memory and bandwidth constraints. Those limits
      encourage short delays, carefully tuned feedback, restricted bandwidth, and compact
      control sets that become part of early digital reverb's recognizable sound.}
    \verbxChronRow{Early 1970s}{Feedback delay network concepts emerge from work on coupled
      digital delay systems and multichannel reverberation. Cross-coupling delay states
      offers greater richness than independent parallel combs. Matrix structure brings new
      questions of stability, energy preservation, eigenvalues, mode distribution, and
      computationally efficient mixing.}
    \verbxChronRow{1970s spatial audio}{Michael Gerzon and collaborators develop Ambisonic
      theory for representing and reproducing a sound field independently of one fixed
      loudspeaker feed. Reverberation now has directional order: early reflections, diffuse
      energy, source rotation, decoding, and listening-area behavior can be expressed within
      a spatial representation. The work supplies conceptual foundations for later surround,
      higher-order Ambisonics, binaural conversion, and scene-based reverberation.}
    \verbxChronRow{1976}{The EMT 250 becomes a landmark commercial digital reverberator.
      Memory-based processing, converters, program selection, and a distinctive tactile
      control surface turn abstract algorithms into a studio instrument. Digital reverb can
      now be recalled and adjusted without moving microphones, changing plates, or occupying
      a purpose-built chamber.}
    \verbxChronRow{Late 1970s}{The Lexicon 224 lineage brings influential modulated digital
      room and plate programs into major studios. Modulation helps break up fixed resonances
      and gives tails motion, but excessive depth can reveal chorus or pitch wandering.
      Remote-console operation anticipates the separation between an engine and its
      production-facing control surface.}
    \verbxChronRow{1979}{James A. Moorer's
      \textquotedblleft{}About This Reverberation Business\textquotedblright{} surveys and
      extends practical digital reverberator design. The discussion treats early reflections,
      comb and allpass structures, low-pass feedback, mode density, and the relation between
      measurable structure and audible character. Its continuing pedagogical value lies in
      connecting implementation choices to listening consequences: delay topology is never
      merely a block diagram when every recirculation changes density, spectrum, and time.}
    \verbxChronRow{Early 1980s}{AMS RMX16, Quantec, Yamaha, Lexicon, and other systems expand
      program-oriented reverberation, nonlinear envelopes, room simulation, and multichannel
      possibilities. Named programs become a practical vocabulary: hall, room, plate,
      ambience, reverse, and nonlinear describe production intentions even when the internal
      structures differ substantially.}
    \verbxChronRow{Early--mid 1980s}{Gated and reverse-style reverbs move the wet envelope
      into the foreground of arrangement. Noise gates, printed room microphones, digital
      nonlinear programs, reversed tapes, and transient-aware routing create tails that rise,
      terminate abruptly, or occupy only selected beats. Reverberation becomes rhythmic
      orchestration rather than passive environmental realism.}
    \verbxChronRow{Mid-to-late 1980s}{Affordable racks such as Yamaha SPX and Alesis systems
      bring multiple digital spaces to project studios, live rigs, and individual musicians.
      MIDI program change and automation improve repeatability. Limited converters, short
      memory, coarse parameters, and characteristic algorithms become audible aesthetics
      rather than defects to be forgotten.}
    \verbxChronRow{1980s sampling and IR experiments}{Longer digital storage encourages
      sampled responses, cross-synthesis, and laboratory convolution even before routine
      low-latency native processing is practical. An impulse response can be edited like
      recorded material: trimmed, windowed, equalized, reversed, resampled, or combined.
      Those freedoms also introduce methodological risks. A transformed IR may be musically
      useful while no longer documenting the room, device, source position, or microphone
      geometry named by its filename.}
    \verbxChronRow{Late 1980s--early 1990s}{Multi-effects processors combine reverb with
      pitch shifting, chorus, delay, gating, dynamics, and modulation. Shimmer-like and
      impossible-room ideas arise from feedback paths that no physical enclosure would
      produce. The distinction between \textquotedblleft{}room simulator\textquotedblright{}
      and \textquotedblleft{}spatial instrument\textquotedblright{} becomes
      increasingly explicit.}
    \verbxChronRow{1992}{Jean-Marc Jot's analysis/synthesis framework connects perceptual
      controls to realtime artificial reverberation. Frequency-dependent decay, statistical
      time-frequency behavior, and a more independent treatment of coloration and duration
      move beyond ad hoc tuning. Reverb design becomes easier to specify in terms meaningful
      to listeners and production engineers.}
    \verbxChronRow{1993}{Realtime binaural concert-hall simulation research joins room
      modeling to headphone presentation. A reverberant field must now preserve direction,
      interaural cues, source distance, and listener interpretation, not merely produce a
      convincing monophonic decay. This work anticipates virtual acoustics, games, VR, and
      mobile spatial rendering.}
    \verbxChronRow{Mid-1990s}{Faster processors and native audio systems support longer
      networks, more modulation, multiband damping, surround outputs, and increasingly
      detailed parameter automation. Algorithm design and user-interface design begin to
      separate: many internal coefficients may be controlled through a smaller perceptual
      parameter set.}
    \verbxChronRow{1996}{Time-frequency analysis and synthesis of room reverberation support
      restoration of impulse responses corrupted by measurement noise. Decay estimation,
      noise-floor handling, and model-based reconstruction connect room measurement to
      synthetic tail generation. The impulse response becomes both evidence and editable
      source material.}
    \verbxChronRow{1997}{Work by Jot and Chaigne, and by Rocchesso and Smith, sharpens FDN
      design through diffuseness, matrix structure, and modal analysis. Stability alone is
      not enough: a network can be mathematically bounded yet ring metallically. Delay
      selection, feedback eigenstructure, damping, and input-output projection jointly
      determine audible quality.}
    \verbxChronRow{Late 1990s}{Long convolution becomes more practical as FFT methods,
      partitioning, memory, and processor performance improve. Measured rooms and devices
      can travel as impulse-response files. The method preserves one linear response with
      high specificity, but source position, listener position, level dependence, motion,
      and nonlinear hardware behavior remain outside an ordinary fixed convolution.}
    \verbxChronRow{1997 onward}{ISO 3382 and related standards consolidate definitions and
      procedures for measuring room-acoustic parameters in performance spaces and, in later
      parts and revisions, other room classes. EDT, T20, T30, clarity, definition, strength,
      and spatial measures describe different evidence rather than interchangeable quality
      scores. Standardization improves comparison, but a report remains incomplete without
      source, receiver, occupancy, bandwidth, noise-floor, fitting, and uncertainty details.}
    \verbxChronRow{2000}{The exponential sine-sweep method associated with Angelo Farina
      provides a robust route from played sweep and recorded room response to an impulse
      response, while separating harmonic distortion responses in time. Measurement moves
      closer to an accessible production workflow, and deconvolution becomes standard
      vocabulary for capturing spaces and devices.}
    \verbxChronRow{Early 2000s}{Native convolution reverbs and growing IR libraries make
      concert halls, chambers, plates, springs, cabinets, and unusual spaces available as
      recallable software assets. Fast convolution scheduling must balance direct heads,
      short early partitions, long late partitions, CPU cost, memory, and monitored latency.
      Provenance becomes part of good library practice.}
    \verbxChronRow{2000s fast convolution}{Nonuniform partitioned convolution separates an
      inexpensive low-latency head from progressively larger tail partitions. The scheduling
      pattern makes long stereo and multichannel responses usable in interactive systems
      without assigning one giant transform to every audio block. Fast convolution is an
      algorithmic family, not a promise of zero latency: block size, partition plan, channel
      count, transform implementation, device transfer, and host buffering still determine
      end-to-end behavior.}
    \verbxChronRow{2000s}{Plug-in abundance ends the practical scarcity of one shared studio
      reverberator. Every track can host a different space, but unlimited instances can
      weaken spatial coherence. Production practice responds with shared buses, early/late
      separation, return equalization, ducking, automation, and deliberate use of a small
      family of related rooms.}
    \verbxChronRow{2000s speech processing}{Dereverberation develops through inverse
      filtering, spectral enhancement, multichannel prediction, source separation, and
      statistical room models. The goal differs from creative reverb removal: speech systems
      optimize intelligibility, recognition, localization, or estimation under assumptions
      about stationarity, microphones, noise, and room-response length.}
    \verbxChronRow{2010s}{Weighted prediction error and related multichannel methods become
      influential for blind speech dereverberation. Delayed linear prediction estimates late
      reverberation while attempting to preserve the desired direct and early components.
      Window length, delay, model order, channel geometry, and source motion define the
      boundary between suppression and damage.}
    \verbxChronRow{2012}{The survey \emph{Fifty Years of Artificial Reverberation} gathers
      the field's development from Schroeder structures through FDNs, convolution,
      modulation, and perceptual approaches. Historical synthesis matters because familiar
      labels often conceal different mechanisms; the same audible goal may be reached by
      physical, statistical, recursive, or measured-response methods.}
    \verbxChronRow{2010s immersive audio}{Ambisonics, object audio, binaural rendering, and
      height-channel formats make reverberation a scene component rather than a stereo insert.
      Early reflections need directional plausibility; late fields need envelopment and
      decorrelation; metadata and channel ordering become part of correctness. Fold-down and
      headphone translation expose errors hidden by a large loudspeaker array.}
    \verbxChronRow{2012 onward}{Cinema and broadcast object-audio workflows, including Dolby
      Atmos production, increase the practical demand for reverberation that scales across
      beds, objects, heights, binaural renderers, and changing playback layouts. A convincing
      mix must survive more than one loudspeaker count. Send design, object width, decorrelated
      late energy, LFE policy, metadata, renderer behavior, and stereo fold-down all become
      parts of the reverberation decision.}
    \verbxChronRow{2015}{Scattering delay network research derives efficient room synthesis
      from geometry-linked delay paths and scattering nodes. First-order reflections can be
      represented accurately while higher orders become progressively coarser. The approach
      occupies a useful middle ground between image-source precision and statistical FDN
      efficiency.}
    \verbxChronRow{Mid-to-late 2010s}{GPU processing, vectorized FFT libraries, and larger
      memory systems make dense multichannel convolution and corpus-scale rendering more
      accessible. Acceleration changes throughput but not representation: unnecessary routing
      paths, oversized transforms, device transfers, and poor partition schedules can still
      dominate cost or create unacceptable realtime latency.}
    \verbxChronRow{Late 2010s}{Neural networks estimate room parameters, map reverberant
      speech toward cleaner targets, generate or transform impulse responses, and emulate
      devices from examples. Data introduces a new acoustic medium: rooms and hardware may
      be compressed into model weights. Dataset coverage, licensing, determinism, and
      disclosure become engineering concerns alongside spectral and temporal quality.}
    \verbxChronRow{Late 2010s--early 2020s}{Realtime ray tracing and geometric-acoustics
      engines become increasingly practical for interactive media. Image sources, stochastic
      rays, beam tracing, and path validation estimate specular and diffuse arrivals from
      scene geometry, while a statistical or recursive tail supplies affordable density.
      CAD ingestion does not solve acoustics automatically: watertight geometry, scale,
      material absorption and scattering, source directivity, diffraction, and listener
      motion remain modeling decisions that must be validated against measurement.}
    \verbxChronRow{Early 2020s}{Differentiable DSP and hybrid neural structures retain
      explicit delays, filters, feedback, convolution, or physical states while learning
      selected parameters and residual behavior. This can improve interpretability and
      stability compared with an unconstrained black box, while still permitting data-driven
      fitting of complex plate, spring, room, or dereverberation behavior.}
    \verbxChronRow{2020s physical simulation}{Finite-element, boundary-element, finite-
      difference, digital-waveguide, ray-tracing, and hybrid solvers benefit from improved
      computation and CAD workflows. No solver removes scale limits: wavelength, mesh
      resolution, boundary models, source directivity, and listener motion determine cost.
      Practical systems divide early geometry, low modes, and statistical late decay.}
    \verbxChronRow{2020s Audio AI}{Synthetic and measured reverberation become tools for data
      augmentation, domain randomization, robustness testing, and spatial learning. Training,
      validation, and test partitions must avoid room leakage and duplicated IR families.
      Reverb parameters, random seeds, source licenses, model versions, and analysis reports
      become part of reproducible dataset provenance.}
    \verbxChronRow{2020s creative practice}{Musicians combine convolution onsets, algorithmic
      tails, freeze, shimmer, reverse envelopes, gates, ducking, resonators, microtonal scale
      emphasis, and immersive motion. Historical categories become modular layers rather
      than mutually exclusive products. The artistic question shifts from
      \textquotedblleft{}Which room?\textquotedblright{} to
      \textquotedblleft{}How should acoustic memory evolve through this phrase?\textquotedblright{}}
    \verbxChronRow{2026}{verbx assembles algorithmic and convolution reverberation,
      dereverberation, measurement, JSON analysis, long and infinite tails, Scala-tuned
      resonance, surround and immersive routing, CLI automation, and AUv3/VST3 preparation
      in one documented system. Its design treats historical vocabulary as testable behavior
      rather than as preset marketing.}
    \verbxChronRow{Beyond 2026}{The likely direction is hybrid: measured early responses,
      geometry-aware paths, stable parametric late networks, learned material or residual
      models, listener-aware spatial rendering, and machine-readable provenance. The durable
      criteria remain musical and scientific: articulation, stability, controllability,
      spatial coherence, reproducibility, attribution, and the ability to make a decay serve
      the form of a sound.}
  \end{longtable}
  \endgroup

  This chronology deliberately ends without declaring a winner. Chambers retain physical
  complexity; plates and springs retain characteristic matter; recursive networks retain
  controllability; convolution retains measured specificity; physical models retain causal
  geometry; learned systems retain interpolation capacity. The history of reverberation is
  therefore not a march toward invisibility. It is an expanding collection of ways to make
  acoustic memory audible, editable, spatial, measurable, and musical.

  proceed through Schroeder's comb and allpass work, feedback delay network analysis,
  perceptual and statistical approaches associated with Jean-Marc Jot and collaborators,

  license rather than establish a DSP claim.

\newcommand{\verbxAfterword}{%
  \clearpage
  \chapter*{Afterword}
  \addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Afterword}
  Reverberation begins as persistence: energy leaves a source, encounters a world, and
  returns changed. Every technique in this book chooses what part of that encounter to
  preserve. A measured impulse response preserves one source-receiver relationship. An
  algorithm preserves selected temporal and spectral statistics. A physical model
  preserves geometry and boundary behavior at a chosen resolution. A learned model
  preserves patterns represented in data. A musician may preserve none of these
  literally and instead use decay, resonance, reversal, gating, or spatial motion as
  independent compositional materials.

  The command line and the plug-in embody complementary ways of working. The CLI favors
  reproducibility, inspection, batch rendering, datasets, reports, and unusual parameter
  ranges. The plug-in favors touch, automation, immediate comparison, and performance
  inside a DAW. Neither interface is the definition of the instrument. The durable center
  is a set of audible transformations whose assumptions can be measured, documented,
  repeated, and deliberately broken.

  Future reverberation systems will likely combine measured spaces, parametric networks,
  geometry, scene metadata, differentiable models, and listener-aware rendering. Their
  success should not be judged by novelty alone. The useful questions remain old ones:
  Does the sound retain articulation? Does the room support the phrase? Does the decay
  reveal or conceal structure? Does the spatial field remain convincing when the listener
  moves? Most importantly, does the result invite another kind of music?

\newcommand{\verbxErrata}{%
  \chapter*{Errata and Corrigenda}
  \addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Errata and Corrigenda}
  Corrections belong to the edition in which an error appears. Before reporting one,
  record the edition date from the copyright page, the PDF page number, the printed page
  number when different, the chapter or appendix heading, and the shortest quotation that
  identifies the passage. For equations, figures, tables, commands, and citations, include
  the corrected form and enough surrounding context to distinguish a typographical error
  from a change in software behavior.

  The live correction record is maintained with the source repository at
  \url{https://github.com/TheColby/verbx/issues}. Use an issue title beginning with
  \texttt{Book erratum:} and include the verbx version or commit when the correction
  concerns generated CLI output. Confirmed corrections are applied to the source files,
  protected by tests when practical, and incorporated into the next generated edition.
  Earlier PDFs remain historical artifacts; the edition date and repository history make
  the correction trail auditable.

  A \emph{corrigendum} records a substantive correction to reasoning, data, attribution,
  or technical instruction. An \emph{erratum} records a smaller production error such as
  a misspelling, malformed symbol, incorrect page reference, or broken link. Suggestions,
  feature requests, and changes caused by later software releases should be filed
  separately so the correction record remains precise.
  \clearpage
}

  This digital book was written in 2026 and updated on July 26, 2026. It was assembled
  from Markdown and repository assets by
  \texttt{scripts\_generate\_docs\_pdf.py}. Pandoc 3.8.2 assembled the manuscript;
  XeLaTeX and TeX Live 2025 produced the PDF; MakeIndex generated the back-of-book index;
  and TikZ drew the operational-card illustrations as resolution-independent vectors.

  The text face is TeX Gyre Schola, an open typeface in the New Century Schoolbook
  tradition. Its OpenType common ligatures are enabled throughout proportional prose,
  headings, captions, tables, front matter, and the index. Menlo is used without ligatures
  for commands and code so technical literals remain exact. The page is U.S. Letter with a book
  hierarchy, roman-numbered front matter, arabic-numbered main matter, lettered appendices,
  and two-column index. Tables use alternating neutral fills; diagrams use the verbx teal,
  gold, rust, blue, plum, and sage palette.

  The illustrated atlas is generated by \texttt{scripts/generate\_userguide\_figures.py}.
  The 588 operational cards are generated by \texttt{scripts\_generate\_plugin\_guide.py}
  and composed one card per page with one of thirteen semantic vector-diagram families.
