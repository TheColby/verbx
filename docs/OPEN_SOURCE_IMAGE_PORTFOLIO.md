# Open-Source Acoustic Image Portfolio

Reverberation is heard, but it is also designed, built, notated, photographed, and remembered. This chapter gathers twenty-five rights-cleared score pages, manuscript leaves, architectural photographs, and images of physical reverberation devices. Together they connect musical texture to the spaces and mechanisms that prolong it. Every image is stored locally so that the guide remains reproducible offline, every figure has a source and license directly beneath its caption, and the complete provenance ledger follows in [`assets/open_source_portfolio/ATTRIBUTIONS.md`](assets/open_source_portfolio/ATTRIBUTIONS.md).

Two requested twentieth-century examples require a firm rights boundary. Aaron Copland's *Fanfare for the Common Man* (1942) and Olivier Messiaen's *Apparition de l'église éternelle* (1932) remain protected works in relevant jurisdictions, and no permissively licensed opening-score reproductions were located. They are therefore discussed elsewhere in this guide but are not reproduced here. The Dukas figure uses the public-domain fanfare from *La Péri* (1911) as a legally reusable orchestral analogue; the Franck figure uses the public-domain *Choral No. 3* (1890) as an organ-writing analogue. This substitution preserves the listening and production lesson without treating online availability as permission.

The portfolio is arranged in three sequences. The first reads notation as a spatial plan: entrances, rests, registral layers, and antiphonal groups imply an acoustic before a note sounds. The second reads buildings as filters whose volume, materials, and geometry govern early reflections and decay. The third opens the machinery of artificial reverberation, from vibrating metal to folded acoustic paths and deliberately reflective test rooms.

### Historical timeline and extended viewing guide

The dates below distinguish the age of a composition, building, or device from the date
of its particular photograph or edition. They also prevent an attractive image from
standing in for history: each figure represents a specific technology, institution, or
musical practice whose acoustic meaning changed over time.

1. Tallis's *Spem in alium* (c. 1570) belongs to the late Tudor Renaissance; the pictured Philip Legge full-score edition dates from 2010 and exposes the forty-part tutti in modern engraving.
2. Machaut's *Messe de Nostre Dame* (c. 1365) belongs to fourteenth-century Ars Nova practice, long before modern concert-hall listening separated musical work from liturgical enclosure.
3. The Waseda chant leaf transmits a medieval notational tradition in which text, breath, ritual pace, and resonant architecture jointly organized time.
4. The Spanish chant manuscript is a hand-produced historical object whose inks, ruling, wear, and irregular script locate music in material culture rather than abstract data.
5. *Ave verum* (traditional; date unknown) uses square notation inherited from medieval chant transmission, making phrase shape more visible than modern bar-based meter.
6. Palestrina's *Missa Papae Marcelli* (c. 1562) is a late-Renaissance polyphonic monument whose sustained contrapuntal entries are highly sensitive to low-mid reverberant buildup.
7. Dukas's *Fanfare pour précéder La Péri* (1912) comes from the early twentieth century and tests whether synchronized brass attacks remain monumental without losing edge definition.
8. Franck's *Choral No. 3* (1890), from the late Romantic organ repertory, treats instrument and church as a coupled sustained-tone system.
9. Gabrieli's *In ecclesiis* (c. 1615) stands at the late-Renaissance and early-Baroque boundary, where separated ensembles and Venetian architecture made placement compositional.
10. The present St. Mark's Basilica was begun in the eleventh century and developed over subsequent centuries; its domes, galleries, and hard ornamented surfaces embody a long-lived coupled-volume acoustic.
11. Notre-Dame de Paris was built principally from the twelfth through fourteenth centuries; its repeated Gothic bays create sequential lateral and overhead reflection zones rather than one simple cavity.
12. Laon Cathedral was built chiefly during the second half of the twelfth and early thirteenth centuries; its nave supplies a clear longitudinal axis with side-aisle and vault paths.
13. Hagia Sophia was completed in 537 during the Byzantine period; its dome and semidomes demonstrate why a visually centralized enclosure can produce acoustically distributed energy.
14. Hamilton Mausoleum was constructed in the mid-nineteenth century; its hard circular interior became known for persistent discrete echoes as well as long decay.
15. The Inchindown oil tanks were built during the Second World War; their vast hard-walled industrial volumes became accidental laboratories for extraordinary reverberation.
16. *Silophone* (2000–2001), by The User, transformed four empty chambers in Montréal's Silo No. 5 into a remotely playable public instrument. Participants sent sound by telephone or web; loudspeakers projected it into the silo, microphones captured the response, and the return was broadcast back to listeners.
17. York Minster's present Gothic fabric developed from the thirteenth through fifteenth centuries, combining a long nave, transepts, tower volume, furnishings, and changing occupancy.
18. The EMT 140, introduced in 1957, converted an electromechanically driven steel plate into a compact studio decay whose density did not depend on a room-sized air volume.
19. Spring reverberators developed through twentieth-century communications, organ, guitar-amplifier, and studio practice; the exposed tank makes wave propagation and transducer coupling physically visible.
20. The Doepfer A-199 belongs to contemporary modular-synthesizer culture, where an external spring tank turns historical electromechanics into a voltage-controlled performance material.
21. The O. C. Electronics folded-line exterior represents a mid-twentieth-century attempt to package a long acoustic propagation path into practical equipment dimensions.
22. The corresponding interior reveals that the folded line was literal geometry: repeated path turns traded compact size against bandwidth, loss, and audible resonant character.
23. The TU Dresden echo chamber photograph dates from 2009, but the room continues the twentieth-century laboratory tradition of deliberately reflective spaces for repeatable acoustic testing.
24. NASA's Reverberant Acoustic Test Facility is a modern aerospace chamber whose horn array and hard enclosure create extreme diffuse acoustic loading for spacecraft qualification, not musical ambience.
25. The IETR chamber is a modern electromagnetic reverberation facility rather than an acoustic room; it is included as a caution that identical terminology can name physically different wave domains.

## Scores, Manuscripts, and Spatial Notation

Thomas Tallis's *Spem in alium* (c. 1570) is an ideal threshold because its forty independent parts make space structurally audible. The following figure replaces the sparse opening with a later climactic page on which all eight five-part choirs are active. Read downward to compare the simultaneous choirs; read horizontally to see sustained tones, imitative entries, and cadential motion overlap in time. In performance, choir placement turns that polyphonic density into motion around the listener. The full-page reproduction deliberately preserves all forty staves because the disposition of active voices is itself a spatial score.

Composed in the late Tudor period, probably around 1570, the motet belongs to a Renaissance culture of large ceremonial vocal polyphony while exceeding ordinary ensemble scale. The page is especially instructive for reverberation design because every new attack enters a field already carrying many decays. A successful long-room treatment must unite the mass without erasing consonants or reducing forty lines to one broadband pad. Compare the page at the direct sound, early-reflection, and late-tail levels: the notation asks each layer to support a different part of the musical argument.

Guillaume de Machaut's *Messe de Nostre Dame* (c. 1365) represents a different relationship between line and room. Figure 17-2 is a modern public-domain score rather than a manuscript facsimile, making the contrapuntal layers immediately legible. Long values, staggered syllables, and voice crossings leave acoustic room between attacks while allowing sustained energy to overlap. When auditioning this repertoire through verbx, begin with moderate early-reflection density and a long but frequency-shaped tail; excessive broadband sustain can erase the rhythmic distinction between the voices that the notation works so carefully to preserve.

Gregorian chant is often described as a single melodic line, but the physical page and the performance space supply additional dimensions. Figure 17-3 shows a chant leaf preserved by Waseda University Library. The compact notation block sits within a broad field of page material, reminding the reader that pacing is carried by breath, text, liturgy, and architecture rather than by a modern metrical grid. For reverb design, chant is a stringent test: the tail should connect phrases and magnify the contour without smearing consonants into an undifferentiated wash.

The manuscript leaf in Figure 17-4 brings material history closer. Red and black inks separate structural and textual functions, while the irregular surface and hand-shaped characters resist the visual uniformity of typesetting. A reverberant realization should retain that human-scale articulation. In practical terms, use enough pre-delay to keep each new phrase distinct, attenuate excessive low-mid accumulation, and compare the dry and wet versions at matched loudness. The goal is not to manufacture an imaginary universal cathedral but to hear how a line acquires dimensionality when reflections remain intelligible.

Figure 17-5 presents *Ave verum* (traditional; date unknown) in square-note notation. The visual grouping of neumes encourages the performer to think in gestures rather than isolated pitches, an idea that transfers directly to reverb listening. Evaluate whether the acoustic preserves the beginning, trajectory, and release of each gesture. If only the release remains audible, the wet path is too dominant; if no connection survives between groups, the decay or early field may be too short. This page is particularly useful for testing a small chapel preset against a large basilica preset while holding performance and loudness constant.

The opening of Giovanni Pierluigi da Palestrina's *Missa Papae Marcelli* (c. 1562) in Figure 17-6 makes imitative density visible. Each voice enters into an already resonating contrapuntal field, so room decay changes not only apparent distance but also the perceived independence of lines. Use this page as a mixing exercise: increase decay until the imitation becomes difficult to follow, then recover clarity with high-frequency damping, low-frequency decay control, and a modest pre-delay rather than simply reducing the wet level. That process separates spectral congestion from reverberant envelopment.

César Franck's *Choral No. 3* (1890) offers a public-domain organ analogue for studying the acoustic questions raised by Messiaen's *Apparition de l'église éternelle* (1932). Organ and room form a coupled system: held tones continuously feed the acoustic, while release reveals the decay uncontaminated by a new attack. Giovanni Gabrieli's *In ecclesiis* (c. 1615) closes the notation sequence because its antiphonal conception points directly toward architectural placement. Figure 17-9 separates vocal and instrumental groups across the page, making alternation, overlap, and tutti arrival visually explicit. A spatial render can honor that design by giving groups distinguishable early-reflection signatures while allowing the late field to unify them. Do not confuse separation with dryness: the most persuasive result often combines localized direct sound, different short reflection patterns, and one shared enveloping decay.

## Resonant Architecture

The interior of St. Mark's Basilica in Figure 17-10 supplies an architectural counterpart to Gabrieli's notation. Galleries, domes, stone surfaces, and separated performance positions create multiple paths between source and listener. The photograph should not be read as a complete acoustic model, but it identifies the geometry a model must explain: strong local reflections, coupled volumes, long paths, and a diffuse late field. When building a St. Mark's-inspired preset, avoid representing the entire space with RT60 alone; combine pre-delay, reflection clusters, frequency-dependent decay, and multichannel placement.

Figure 17-11 looks upward through the ribbed vaults of Notre-Dame de Paris. Repeated bays establish a sequence of reflection zones rather than one undifferentiated cavity. Stone boundaries support long decay, but pillars, ribs, furnishings, and occupied surfaces scatter and absorb energy unevenly. This is why a cathedral preset benefits from temporal texture: perfectly smooth exponential decay can sound less plausible than a tail with evolving density and gentle modal variation. The photograph also encourages vertical thinking for immersive mixes, where height returns can carry vault energy without pulling the direct source upward.

Laon Cathedral's nave in Figure 17-12 makes source-listener distance easy to imagine. The central aisle supplies a long direct axis, side aisles create lateral paths, and the high vault supports delayed overhead energy. In a stereo simulation, lateral reflections often contribute more apparent width than an indiscriminate increase in wet level. In surround or Atmos-oriented production, route early energy according to plausible directions, then let the late field broaden across sides, rears, and heights. The visual symmetry is useful, but the acoustic result should still include small asymmetries that prevent a sterile mirror image.

The dome of Hagia Sophia in Figure 17-13 demonstrates a radically different visual organization: a vast central volume surrounded by layered surfaces and openings. Domes can focus, redistribute, and prolong sound in ways that a rectangular-room model cannot reproduce literally. For creative reverb, the lesson is not to imitate one measured impulse response from appearance alone. Instead, use geometry-informed reflection timing, a dense late network, and restrained modulation to evoke scale while preserving musical control. Compare mono, stereo, and immersive versions to hear how overhead energy changes perceived enclosure.

Hamilton Mausoleum, shown in Figure 17-14, is famous for an exceptionally persistent echo. Its circular plan, hard surfaces, and domed ceiling create a compact but highly reflective system. The image is a reminder that long audible persistence does not require cathedral-scale floor area; geometry and absorption are decisive. In verbx, compare a smooth long RT60 preset with a sparse repeating-echo design. The latter may better communicate the perceptual identity of a space in which discrete returns remain audible before the response becomes diffuse.

The Inchindown oil-storage tunnel in Figure 17-15 leads toward enormous underground tanks known for extremely long decay. York Minster's nave in Figure 17-17 completes the architectural sequence with a broad frontal view. ## Mechanical and Laboratory Reverberation

The EMT 140 in Figure 17-18 makes plate reverberation physically legible. An electromechanical driver excites a large suspended steel plate, pickups capture its vibration, and damping changes the decay. The device is not a miniature room; it is a distributed resonant surface whose dense modal response produces a smooth, characteristic tail. When designing a plate-like algorithm, prioritize rapid density growth, controlled low-frequency behavior, and a bright but non-grainy decay. The photograph also explains why a classic plate occupies substantial physical space even though a plug-in reduces it to a few controls.

![EMT 140 plate reverberator.](assets/open_source_portfolio/18_emt_140_plate.jpg)

**Figure: EMT 140 plate reverberator, showing the large framed steel-plate assembly used to create dense artificial decay.**

*Source and license:* EMT-Archiv-Lahr, [Wikimedia Commons file record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:EMT_140.jpg), [CC BY-SA 4.0](https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0/). Reproduced without alteration except scaling.

Figure 17-19 removes the enclosure from a spring-reverb tank so its transducers and parallel springs are visible. A driver launches torsional waves into the springs; reflections, dispersion, and coupling produce the familiar lively, sometimes “drippy” response. Spring behavior is strongly source-dependent: impulsive guitar or percussion reveals boing and chirp, while sustained material can become a diffuse halo. A digital model should expose enough control over dispersion, damping, and nonlinear drive to move between subtle coloration and obvious effect rather than treating every spring as one fixed preset.

The compact Doepfer A-199 spring assembly in Figure 17-20 shows how the same principle scales into modular synthesis. The mechanical element is modest, but feedback routing, equalization, and excitation level can turn it into a playable resonator. This suggests a productive plug-in design principle: physical-model parameters should remain available on an Expert page while performance macros expose musically stable combinations. For a spring preset, map one macro to input drive and damping together, another to feedback and tone, and retain a safety limiter because resonant feedback can rise rapidly.

Figure 17-21 identifies an O. C. Electronics Folded-Line reverberation device from its exterior label. Folded acoustic or mechanical paths pursue a familiar engineering goal: obtain a longer effective propagation path inside a compact enclosure. The label is visually modest, but it introduces an important conceptual bridge from physical delay lines to digital delay networks. In both cases, topology determines recurrence. A model becomes more convincing when delays, feedback gains, filtering, and coupling are treated as a system rather than as independent decorative controls.

The opened device in Figure 17-22 reveals the internal folded element alluded to by the label. This paired view is useful because it distinguishes product description from mechanism. The TU Dresden echo chamber in Figure 17-23 is an intentionally reflective acoustic room. Irregularly oriented panels disrupt simple parallel-wall paths and help distribute energy through many directions. Such a chamber is useful for material measurements and for generating dense reverberant fields under controlled conditions. The image clarifies why diffusion is not synonymous with decay: scattering changes the direction and density of reflections, while absorption and volume primarily govern energy loss. In verbx, adjust diffusion while holding RT60 constant to hear that distinction directly.

Figure 17-24 shows the horn room of a reverberant acoustic test facility. This is not a musical echo chamber: its powerful horns create controlled high-level diffuse sound fields for aerospace testing. The distinction is instructive. Reverb metrics describe the response of a source-room-receiver system, so source directivity, bandwidth, level, and measurement position matter. A room that is excellent for qualification testing may be unusable for music, while its measurement discipline remains valuable. When validating an algorithm, specify stimulus, level, sample rate, channel geometry, and analysis window rather than reporting RT60 alone.

The IETR chamber in Figure 17-25 is an electromagnetic reverberation chamber, not an audio room. It belongs here as a carefully labeled comparative image: angled conducting surfaces and stirring structures create a statistically distributed field for radio-frequency measurements. The analogy to acoustic diffusion is conceptually useful, but the wave impedances, boundary conditions, transducers, and frequency scales are different. Keeping that boundary explicit is good scientific practice. The shared lesson is that a “diffuse field” is an experimental objective produced by geometry, source behavior, and averaging, not a visual style or a single reverb knob.
